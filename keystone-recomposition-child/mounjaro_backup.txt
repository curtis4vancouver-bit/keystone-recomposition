			2. The DEXA Scan Twist: Skeletal Muscle vs. Organ Volume<br>
		</h2>
<p style="margin-bottom: 25px;">
			While the raw data is concerning, it requires a critical physiological translation. A common mistake among health commentators is assuming that “lean mass loss” on a Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry (DEXA) scan translates entirely to shredded skeletal muscle.
		</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 25px;">
			<strong>It does not.</strong> Research published by <strong>Dr. Keith Baar at the University of California, Davis</strong>, highlighted a major diagnostic twist. A significant portion of the “lean mass” recorded as lost during GLP-1/GIP therapy is actually derived from <strong>visceral organ shrinkage and localized intracellular water loss</strong>—not purely skeletal muscle.
		</p>
<ul style="margin-bottom: 25px; padding-left: 20px; list-style-type: square;">
<li style="margin-bottom: 12px;">
				<strong style="color: #C4A265;">The Liver Effect:</strong> GLP-1 and GIP peptides aggressively clear ectopic fat stored within the liver and surrounding visceral organs. As the liver clears this fat, its physical volume shrinks. On a standard DEXA scan, this visceral volume reduction is categorized as “lean mass loss.”
			</li>
<li style="margin-bottom: 12px;">
				<strong style="color: #C4A265;">Hydration Drawdown:</strong> Rapid drops in caloric and carbohydrate intake lead to glycogen depletion. Since glycogen bonds to water in a 1:3 ratio, depleting glycogen draws a massive amount of water out of intracellular space. This water loss is read as a reduction in lean tissue.
			</li>
</ul>
<p style="margin-bottom: 25px;">
			Even after adjusting for organ volume and water loss, <strong>actual skeletal muscle wasting remains severe.</strong> When your body is placed in an extreme, peptide-enforced caloric deficit, it enters a state of metabolic emergency. If it is not stimulated otherwise, the body will catabolize (digest) its own structural muscle proteins to provide energy.
		</p>
